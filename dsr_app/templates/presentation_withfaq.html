What is Super Donor?

Super Donor is a tool to predict whether distinct sperm donor bank and ID listings might actually represent the same underlying person. 25% of sperm donors report donating to more than one bank, and sperm banks sometimes transfer samples, resulting in multiple listings for the same underlying donor. Super Donor makes this information more accessible and useful for families and donor-conceived people seeking out information about their genetic connections.

How can I use Super Donor?

To use this tool, enter the bank and donor ID for the donor you would like to search. This will produce a rank-ordered list of similar donors and prediction of whether these other donors are “Likely”, “Possibly” or “Unlikely” the same donor.

How should I interpret the predictions?

No predictive tool is perfect, especially a tool like this that is looking for “needles in a haystack.” This tool is only a first step — it cannot ever say definitively whether two donor IDs are truly the same person. If the ranked list produces “Likely” or “Possible” matches for your donor, take a look at their features, look up their full descriptions on the Donor Sibling Registry — do the longer descriptions seem very similar? If you are a person seeking genetic connections, you can consider reaching out to listings under this other donor ID via the DSR messaging system or other means, but remember that the only a genetic test can truly confirm a match.

My list doesn’t produces only “Unlikely” matches, but I know for sure there is an ID on this list that is the same person, what went wrong?

This tool is structured to give a ranked list even when there are no predicted matches because the algorithm is only a first step. Especially if descriptions for donors are not very long, or if there is missing information (e.g. no blood type or eye color), the algorithm can produce an “Unlikely” rating for a true match. You can still seek out more information on the top-ranked donors to see if one of them might be a match. Also, keep in mind that most donors will NOT have a duplicate listing

Why isn't my donor listed?

Super Donor includes donors from 32 banks listed on the Donor Sibling Registry, focused on banks with larger numbers of donors and banks known to have transferred samples between banks. These data were pulled from the Donor Sibling Registry in June 2016, and only donors with sufficient descriptive information for a prediction to be made are included here.

I know of a confirmed match, can I get this information included in Super Donor?

This project relied small training known matches, and works best for donors who are similar in some way to this original training set. The more true matches we know of, the stronger the predictive power of this tool will be. If you know of a confirmed match — distinct donor IDs that represent the same underlying person — please consider contacting me so I can add this information to the model. How does Super Donor work?

Super Donor uses Machine Learning, Natural Language Processing and Metric-Learning to analyze what descriptive words and physical features are most important for determining whether two distinct donor listings represent the same underlying donor. Does this project compromise the privacy of donors or donor-offspring?

This project works only from information already listed publicly on the DSR and does not seek to match donor features to an individual sperm donor. That is, Super Donor detects possible matches between anonymous donors, NOT links between an anonymous donor ID and the identity of the donor himself (which would be a much harder problem, and likely ill-advised from an ethical standpoint). Information about individual donor offspring that could be potentially identifying (e.g. login names or birth month/year) is not included.

Why did you build Super Donor?

I love using Mathematics and Data Science to shine light where information is scarce, noisy and messy — but insight is deeply needed. Despite increasing rates of conception with donor sperm, there is no centralized source of information — there isn’t even an accurate count of how many donor conceived people are born in the US per year. Careful and creative analysis of crowd-sourced data like the DSR can help fill in the gaps and provide real information to people who need it. This project was also a great way to practice Natural Language Processing and Metric-Learning tools on an unwieldy real-world data set, some of my key goals as a participant in the Insight Health Data Science Program in Boston, Summer 2016.
